## Study Plan: Financial Sustainability

### 1. Foundational Financial Literacy

- [ ] The three core financial statements and how they relate to each other
  - [ ] [Profit & Loss (Income Statement)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Income_statement):
        revenue, cost of goods sold, gross profit, operating expenses, net
        income
  - [ ] [Balance Sheet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balance_sheet): assets,
        liabilities, equity
  - [ ] [Cash Flow Statement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cash_flow_statement):
        why profit ≠ cash, operating vs investing vs financing flows
- [ ] [Break-even analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Break-even_point):
      fixed costs, variable costs, contribution margin, break-even volume
- [ ] [Working capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Working_capital): why a
      profitable business can run out of cash

### 2. Unit Economics (the most important section for your pitch)

- [ ] [Unit economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_economics):
      profit/loss per single transaction or customer
- [ ] [Contribution margin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contribution_margin):
      revenue minus variable costs per order
- [ ] [Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Customer_acquisition_cost):
      what it costs to bring in one new consumer/restaurant
- [ ] [Customer Lifetime Value (LTV or CLV)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Customer_lifetime_value):
      total revenue expected from one member over time
- [ ] LTV/CAC ratio: the standard benchmark for marketplace viability
      (typically >3x)
- [ ] Payback period: how long until CAC is recovered

### 3. Marketplace & Platform Economics

- [ ] [Two-sided / multi-sided markets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-sided_market):
      how platforms set prices on each side, cross-side network effects
- [ ] [Network effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_effect): why scale
      matters and the chicken-and-egg problem at launch
- [ ] [Take rate / commission rate](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commission_(remuneration)>):
      how platforms earn per transaction and the ceiling imposed by competition
- [ ] Liquidity: the density problem — minimum orders/drivers needed per area
      for the platform to work
- [ ] [Economies of scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economies_of_scale):
      how unit costs fall as volume grows

### 4. Cost Structure for Food Delivery

- [ ] Fixed costs: software infrastructure, staff salaries, legal/admin,
      insurance
- [ ] Variable costs: payment processing fees (typically 1.5–3%), customer
      support per order, driver coordination overhead
- [ ] Labor models: salaried employees vs gig workers — cost implications and
      how to model each
- [ ] Driver utilization rate: orders per driver per hour as the key operational
      efficiency metric
- [ ] Industry benchmarks: research what incumbents (Uber Eats, Deliveroo, etc.)
      actually report — revenue per order, driver pay per order, restaurant
      commission rates

### 5. Revenue Model

- [ ] Commission-only vs hybrid models (commissions + SaaS fees + consumer fees)
- [ ] Sensitivity analysis: how net income changes as commission rate, order
      volume, or driver wages vary
- [ ] Pricing power: what commission rate can you charge while still being
      cheaper than incumbents?

### 6. Financial Projections & Business Plan

- [ ] [Financial modeling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_modeling):
      building a projection from assumptions (bottom-up vs top-down)
- [ ] Bottom-up forecasting: start from number of restaurants × average
      orders/day × average order value
- [ ] Scenario analysis: base case, pessimistic, optimistic — what assumptions
      drive each
- [ ] [Burn rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burn_rate) and runway: how long
      pre-revenue capital lasts
- [ ] [Operating leverage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operating_leverage):
      how profitability scales with revenue once fixed costs are covered

### 7. Cooperative-Specific Financial Considerations

- [ ] [Cooperative capital structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooperative):
      member shares, retained earnings, how cooperatives raise money without
      equity dilution
- [ ] [Patronage dividends](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooperative#Surplus):
      distributing surplus to members proportional to use
- [ ] Funding sources specific to cooperatives: cooperative development funds,
      solidarity finance, EU/national cooperative support programs
- [ ] How to present financial sustainability without a profit motive to funders
      and prospective members

### 8. Validation & Benchmarking

- [ ] Find and read at least one public cooperative annual report (e.g.,
      Mondragon, REI, a credit union)
- [ ] Find one food delivery competitor's investor report or S-1 filing for real
      cost/revenue benchmarks
- [ ] Compare your projected commission rate and driver wage to incumbent rates
      — quantify the "better deal" claim
